Fixed Points and the Verification Problem

On why checking an AI might be as hard as training one

Brouwer's fixed point theorem says something simple: any continuous function mapping a space to itself always has at least one point that doesn't move. It maps to itself.

Stir a cup of coffee. When it settles, at least one point in the liquid ended up exactly where it started.

I've been thinking about this in the context of RL verification. When a model is being trained, a verifier evaluates whether its outputs are safe. The guardrail. But the model is optimizing against that verifier — not maliciously, but structurally. Training pushes it toward outputs that score well, which isn't always the same as outputs that are actually safe.

Value iteration in RL is already a search for a fixed point — the Bellman operator, stabilizing when it finds a policy that reproduces itself under evaluation. What if that fixed point is one where the model has learned to satisfy the verifier's surface patterns rather than its intent?

A stable, converged, verified-looking policy. That isn't.

I don't know if this is the right lens. But the image stays with me — a system finding stability in exactly the wrong place.

What does it mean to verify something that's optimizing to be verified?

Beyond the Default

On technology as a space to experiment with how we organize

For most of history, social systems weren't really chosen. They emerged, calcified, and became the only thing that felt possible.

Bitcoin changed something about that — not by succeeding at everything it claimed, but by showing that a foundational primitive like money could be rebuilt from scratch and actually run. Flawed, contested. But real.

That moment quietly expanded what feels possible. If money can be reimagined by a pseudonymous person with a whitepaper, what else is sitting there waiting?

The institutions we have were designed for a different world. Not wrong, just not built for this one. And somewhere in that gap there's room for systems that couldn't have existed before — not utopias, just different configurations. Testable ones.

We haven't seriously explored that space yet.

What would we build if we weren't trying to optimize the existing thing?
